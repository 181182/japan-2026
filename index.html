🍣

Raw fish everywhere?

"What if I don't like sushi and everything is raw?"

tap to flip ↻

Most Japanese food isn't raw at all. Ramen, tonkatsu, tempura, yakitori, okonomiyaki, curry — all cooked, all amazing. Konbini have safe favourites 24/7, and plastic food models in windows mean you always see exactly what you're ordering. Sushi is 100% optional.

🗣️

Nobody speaks English?

"How will we order, ask for help, find anything?"

tap to flip ↻

Japan is built for visitors who don't speak Japanese. Stations, menus and signs have English; Google Translate's camera reads anything instantly; and the pocket phrases on this page cover 95% of situations. Locals are famously patient — pointing + smiling works wonders.

🌏

Earthquakes?

"What if the ground starts shaking while we're there?"

tap to flip ↻

Japan is the best-prepared country on Earth. Buildings and hotels are engineered to strict quake codes, phones get automatic early-warning alerts, and small tremors are routine non-events locals barely notice. Emergency numbers are in Essentials — but odds are you'll feel nothing at all.

🚇

Getting lost in the crowds?

"Tokyo has 37 million people and the metro map looks like spaghetti."

tap to flip ↻

Google Maps transit in Japan is flawless — exact platform, exact car, exact price. The IC card just taps in and out, no tickets to decode. Every station has English signs and numbered exits. And we're a team: worst case, the next train is 4 minutes away.

♨️

Onsen etiquette?

"Bathing with strangers, naked, with rules I don't know?"

tap to flip ↻

The rules fit on one hand: wash at the showers first, small towel stays out of the water, hair tied up, no photos. That's it — nobody stares, everyone minds their own bath. Not ready for the shared one? Many places offer private (kashikiri) baths you can book for just us two.

💴

Cash-only confusion?

"Will our cards even work? How much cash do we carry?"

tap to flip ↻

Easier than you think. 7-Eleven ATMs are everywhere, open 24/7, and happily take Norwegian cards. Cities are increasingly card-friendly, the IC card pays for transit, vending machines and konbini. The Money section has the full plan — carrying ~¥10–20k cash covers the rest.

🌡️ Essentials & safety

July is peak summer: 30–35 °C, very humid, and the tail of the rainy season into mid-month. Heat is the single biggest risk on this trip — plan indoor escapes for 1–4pm and drink constantly.

☀️ Heat strategy: aim outdoor sights for before 11am or after 5pm. Carry a water bottle, a cooling towel, and a hand fan; refill at any konbini. Convenience stores, department basements (depachika), museums, and aquariums are your air-conditioned reset points. Watch for heatstroke — dizziness, headache, stopped sweating means stop, cool down, and rehydrate with salt (pocari/aquarius).

Lost item? Japan's lost-and-found is excellent — report at the nearest station office or kōban (police box).

🎎 Etiquette & customs — quick wins

  • No tipping — it can confuse or offend. Great service is standard.
  • Shoes off at ryokan, some restaurants, temples, fitting rooms — watch for a step up or slippers.
  • Pay on the tray, not into the hand. Cash still rules small spots.
  • Trains are quiet — phone on silent, no calls. No eating on local trains (shinkansen is fine).
  • Escalators: stand left in Tokyo, right in Osaka.
  • Slurping noodles is polite; don't stick chopsticks upright in rice.
  • Don't eat while walking; bin rubbish is rare — carry a small bag.
  • Tattoos can bar entry to some onsen — seek tattoo-friendly or private baths.
A little “sumimasen” (excuse me / thank you) and a small bow go a long way. Convenience-store and station staff are your best friends.

💴 Money & budget

Japan is still surprisingly cash-friendly — many small restaurants, shrines, and lockers are cash-only. Carry ¥10,000–20,000 (≈580–1,160 kr) on you and top up as you go.

💱 NOK conversions shown throughout are locked to the live rate of 31 May 2026: ¥1,000 ≈ 58 kr (¥17.2 = 1 kr — the yen is very weak, great for you). Check today's rate: JPY → NOK.

💳 Cash & cards

Withdraw yen at 7-Eleven ATMs (in every konbini) or Japan Post ATMs — both reliably accept foreign Visa/Mastercard. Notify your bank you'll be in Japan to avoid blocks.

IC cards (Suica/ICOCA) cover trains, buses, konbini and vending — top up with cash at any station machine. Cards/Apple Pay work in chains, but always keep cash for the small places.

🛍️ Tax-free shopping

Spend ¥5,000+ (≈290 kr) in one store in one day and claim ~10% tax-free at the counter — bring your passport. Great for the electronics (Yodobashi) and fabric/craft hauls. Goods are sealed; don't open until you leave Japan. Keep receipts for the customs check on departure.

🎫 JR pass strategy

Your big legs are spread across 18 days, so an all-you-can-ride nationwide pass is poor value. Mix individual tickets with one regional pass instead.

Bottom line: Skip the ~¥100,000 (≈5,800 kr) nationwide pass. Individual tickets for the north + a Kansai-Hiroshima Area Pass for the west ≈ ¥62,000/person (≈3,600 kr). All figures approximate 2026 — verify at booking.

❌ Nationwide JR Pass avoid

21-day pass ≈ ¥100,000 (≈5,800 kr). Even buying every ticket individually (~¥64,700, ≈3,760 kr) beats it by ~¥35,000 (≈2,030 kr). Built for people criss-crossing the country in one week — not your pace.

The Hokuriku Arch Pass (¥35,000 (≈2,030 kr) since Mar 2026) also loses to individual tickets here because you travel that route one-way, not round-trip.

The one catch ⚠️

The Kansai-Hiroshima Pass is 5 consecutive days, so it can't cover both Hiroshima (Jul 14) and the airport run (Jul 20) — 6 days apart. Activate it Jul 14 (it then covers Hiroshima + Miyajima ferry + Nara + Osaka→Kyoto + Kyoto local trains through Jul 18), and buy the final Kyoto→KIX Haruka separately.

💳 IC cards & local transport

One tap-card each for everything off-pass; add a city pass only where it clearly pays.

IC card — get this first

Easiest: add Suica or ICOCA to your phone wallet (especially iPhone) — no queue, no deposit, top up with your card. Physical: grab an ICOCA at KIX on arrival. Works on metros, buses, local trains, konbini, and vending machines nationwide. Start with ¥3,000–5,000 (≈175–290 kr) loaded.

🥃 Breweries & distilleries

Full-scale, proper operations with real guided tours — no basement nano-breweries.

Suntory Yamazaki Distillery book ASAP · lottery

Birthplace of Japanese whisky, maker of Yamazaki single malt. Real mash tuns, washbacks, pot stills and ageing warehouses. On your Osaka–Kyoto line — 10 min walk from JR Yamazaki Station (covered by your pass). Craftsmanship Tour: 80 min, ¥3,000 (≈175 kr), exclusive tastings. Museum + tasting lounge as the backup if tours sell out. English available, 20+ only.

Best slot: a half-day from Kyoto (Jul 18/19), or a stop on the Jul 17 move. Tours run on a monthly lottery — enter the instant July opens.

Hombo Shuzo's highland malt distillery in the Central Alps — one of Japan's highest, makers of the award-winning Iwai & Mars whiskies. Free self-guided tour of the stills + a paid tasting flight and bottle shop. ~1h from Nagano toward Komagane; a great half-day detour for whisky fans.

Japan's first dedicated craft gin distillery, using yuzu, hinoki, gyokuro tea & sansho pepper. Production tours are limited/by appointment, but you can taste the full Ki No Bi range at top Kyoto bars — ask for the flight.

🍸 Cocktail & whisky bars

Japan makes some of the world's best cocktails — precise, quiet, ice carved by hand. Below are standout bars plus a ready-made bar-to-bar crawl for each city. Many seat <10 people; arrive early or book. Expect a small seating/snack charge (otōshi).

Etiquette: at high-end bars keep it low-key — no big groups shouting. A cover charge of ¥500–1,500 (≈30–90 kr) is normal. Cash is safest. "Omakase" lets the bartender build to your taste.
